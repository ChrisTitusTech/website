What people don't understand about Linux is distributions do not matter. People think that if they use a different distribution, they will have a different experience. This is not true. The core of Linux is the same across all distributions. The only difference is the software that is included and the way it is configured.
<!--more-->

## What about a Gaming Distro?

Just like gaming distros, there are "gaming" headsets or "gaming" debloat utilities. Its all a bunch of bullshit. A gaming distro is just a regular distro with some gaming software pre-installed. It does not make it better for gaming. The performance of games on Linux is determined by the hardware and the drivers, not the distribution. There can be custom kernels or custom packages to improve compatibility, but you can always install these on any distribution. Gaming is just a buzzword that companies use to sell more products. It does not have any real meaning in the context of Linux distributions.

The kernel customizations that Bazzite and CachyOS use can be installed on Arch Linux. The only difference is that they have it pre-installed. This does not make it better for gaming. It just means that you have to do a little bit of extra work to get the same performance on Arch Linux.

## The Linux Journey

>> "Its not about the destination, its about the journey" - Anonymous

The Linux journey is about learning and exploring. It is about discovering new things and finding out what works best for you. It is not about finding the perfect distribution or the perfect desktop environment. It is about finding what works for you and enjoying the process of learning and exploring. 

What did my journey look like? 
- November 2018: I started with Ubuntu
- December 2018: I switched to Fedora
- January 5th 2019: I switched to OpenSUSE
- January 15th 2019: I switched to Manjaro
- February 2019: I switched to Arch Linux
- March 2019: I switched to Debian

For the next couple years, I switched between Arch Linux and Debian. Mostly trying different Desktop Environments and then moved to Window Managers instead of the DEs. I have been using Arch Linux for the past 3 years and I have no plans to switch anytime soon. I have found what works for me and I am happy with it.

I really stopped switching when I found that each distro was just a different look with a few more packages installed or configured. The package manager and how things were installed changed a little between distros, but the core of Linux was the same. I realized that I could install any software I wanted on any distribution, and I could configure it to my liking. The only difference was the way it was packaged and the way it was configured by default.

## The Uncomfortable Truths

- Linux users don't want Windows users to switch to Linux. 
- Windows users don't want to learn Linux.
- Companies and developers don't want to support Linux. (It's not profitable for them)
